FAQ

Straight questions need simple answers.

Endometriosis is a chronic condition where tissue similar to the lining of the uterus grows outside the uterus, often on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, or pelvic lining. This tissue behaves like normal endometrial tissue—it thickens, breaks down, and bleeds with each menstrual cycle—but it has no way to exit the body, leading to inflammation, pain, and scar tissue.

The main symptom is pelvic pain, often much worse than normal period cramps. Other common symptoms include painful periods, pain during or after intercourse, pain with bowel movements or urination (especially during periods), heavy menstrual bleeding, infertility, fatigue, bloating, and nausea.

Diagnosis usually starts with a review of symptoms and a pelvic exam. Imaging tests like ultrasound or MRI can help identify cysts. However, the only definitive way to diagnose endometriosis is through laparoscopy—a minimally invasive surgery where a camera is inserted to view and possibly biopsy the tissue.

There is currently no cure for endometriosis, but it can be effectively managed. Treatments include pain medication, hormonal therapies (like birth control pills), and surgery to remove the abnormal tissue. Symptoms often improve after menopause, but management is key to improving quality of life.

Treatments like fertility medications or assisted reproductive technologies (e.g., IVF) can help. Early consultation with a specialist is recommended if pregnancy is a goal.
